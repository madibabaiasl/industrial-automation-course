Module 01 · Principle

Sensing principles and signal interpretation

The core principle for this module, taught one step at a time.

Every automated cell begins with one question: what is the state of the world right now? A sensor answers it by turning a physical quantity — distance, presence, light, force — into an electrical signal the controller can read. This is the sense in sense–decide–act. Work through the steps below; each one appears when you are ready for it.

1 · What a sensor actually outputs

Sensors fall into two families, and the controller treats them very differently. A discrete sensor reports one bit: present or absent, tripped or clear. A limit switch and an inductive proximity sensor are discrete. An analog sensor reports a continuous value, usually a voltage or current that rises and falls with the measured quantity. Knowing which family you are holding decides how you wire it and how you read it in the program.

2 · Sensitivity: how the signal tracks the world

For an analog sensor, the cleanest model is a straight line: the output voltage $V_\text{out}$ is proportional to the measured quantity $x$, plus an offset.

$$ V_\text{out} = S\,x + V_0 $$

Here $V_0$ is the reading at $x = 0$, and the slope $S$ is the sensitivity, defined as the change in output per unit change in the world:

$$ S = \frac{\Delta V_\text{out}}{\Delta x} $$

A larger $S$ gives more signal for a small change, which makes weak effects easier to detect — but push it too far and the output runs into its supply rail and saturates, losing all information above that point.

3 · The trip point, and why one threshold is not enough

To turn an analog reading into a yes/no decision, you compare it against a threshold. The trouble is noise. When $x$ sits near a single threshold, small fluctuations cross it back and forth, and the output chatters on and off many times a second. The fix is hysteresis: use two thresholds instead of one. Turn the output on only when $x > x_\text{on}$, and turn it off only when $x < x_\text{off}$, with $x_\text{on} > x_\text{off}$. The gap $x_\text{on} - x_\text{off}$ is a dead band that noise cannot repeatedly cross.

Check your understanding

A proximity signal hovers right at a single trip threshold while the target vibrates slightly. What appears at the output, and what fixes it?

Near one threshold, noise crosses it repeatedly, so the discrete output toggles rapidly. Two thresholds create a dead band $x_\text{on}-x_\text{off}$ that a small fluctuation cannot cross twice, so the output holds steady.

4 · Putting the numbers together

These two ideas — a linear transfer function and a sensible trip point — are almost everything you need to interpret a sensor on the bench. The last check ties the sensitivity equation to a number.

Check your understanding

A linear sensor has $V_\text{out} = S\,x + V_0$ with sensitivity $S = 20\ \text{mV/mm}$. If $x$ increases by $3\ \text{mm}$, how much does $V_\text{out}$ change?

The change is $\Delta V_\text{out} = S\,\Delta x = 20\ \text{mV/mm} \times 3\ \text{mm} = 60\ \text{mV}$. The offset $V_0$ shifts the whole line but does not affect a change.
