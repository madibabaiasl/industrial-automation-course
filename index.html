The premise

The hardware is not the subject. It is the testbed.

The Allen-Bradley PLC and the MELFA SCARA arm are where you make real the principles used across all industrial automation. Every unit rehearses a principle in open-source simulation, then applies it to a different task on the bench. You are assessed on transfer and on troubleshooting, not on reproducing a memorized sequence.

Four units

The course, in sequence

01Sense

Industrial Sensing

Detecting and interpreting system state. Proximity, photoelectric, and limit sensors; thresholds, false detection, wiring faults, and noisy signals.

Open unit →
02Act

Industrial Actuation

Producing physical action. Motors, relays, solenoids, and the robot's servo axes; output-not-firing, wrong direction, timing mismatch, and unsafe sequence.

Open unit →
03Decide

Industrial Control

Deciding and regulating behavior. PLC sequencing with timers, counters, interlocks, and fault handling; robot motion control as the continuous case.

Open unit →
04Integrate

System Integration

Combining all three into a detect–decide–actuate–verify–handle-faults cell, with PLC–robot handshake and HMI-style status.

How each unit runs

Simulation to hardware

01 · LEARN

Principle first

A short, segmented lesson introduces the principle one step at a time, with practice questions to check understanding.

02 · SIMULATE

Rehearse the logic

You write and run your own control logic in open-source simulation, change parameters, and debug against a live system.

03 · BUILD

Apply on the bench

The same principle, a different task, on the Allen-Bradley PLC and the SCARA arm. Success is transfer, not repetition.
